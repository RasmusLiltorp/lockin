## Spørgsmål 1 (7%) — Which propositions are true?

1. **Problem.** Let $p,q,r,s$ be propositions. Which of 1.a–1.j are true?
2. **Type.** Truth-value evaluation of compound propositions / truth-table sizing.
3. **Method.** For each claim: (1) if it's an implication "if $A$ then $B$", it's false only when $A$ true and $B$ false — check that one case. (2) For "iff", check both directions. (3) For truth-table row counts, a formula with $k$ distinct variables has $2^k$ rows — count the *distinct* letters, not occurrences. Swap in your own formula and repeat.
4. **Worked solution.**
   - **1.a** "If $p\lor q$ is false, then $p$ is false." $p\lor q$ false $\Rightarrow$ both false $\Rightarrow p$ false. **TRUE.**
   - **1.b** "$p\lor q$ false iff $q$ false." Backward fails: $q$ false, $p$ true gives $p\lor q$ true. **FALSE.**
   - **1.c** "If $p$ true and $q$ false, then $p\land q$ false." $p\land q$ = T$\land$F = F. **TRUE.**
   - **1.d** "If $p$ false, then $p\Rightarrow q$ true." False antecedent makes implication vacuously true. **TRUE.**
   - **1.e** "If $p\oplus q$ true and $p$ true, then $q$ true." XOR true + $p$ true $\Rightarrow q$ false. Conclusion wrong. **FALSE.**
   - **1.f** "If $p$ false, then $p\Rightarrow q$ false." Opposite of 1.d; it's actually true. **FALSE.**
   - **1.g** "Table for $p\land q\land r$ has 3 rows." 3 variables $\Rightarrow 2^3=8$ rows. **FALSE.**
   - **1.h** "$(p\land q)\lor(r\land\neg q)$ has 16 rows." Variables $p,q,r$ = 3 $\Rightarrow 8$ rows. **FALSE.**
   - **1.i** "$(p\land q)\lor(r\land s)$ has 16 rows." Variables $p,q,r,s$ = 4 $\Rightarrow 2^4=16$. **TRUE.**
   - **1.j** "If $p\Rightarrow q$ and $\neg p\Rightarrow\neg q$ both true, then $p\Leftrightarrow q$." The two implications give $p\Rightarrow q$ and $q\Rightarrow p$, i.e. equivalence. **TRUE.**
   - **True: 1.a, 1.c, 1.d, 1.i, 1.j.** (checked: Python)

## Spørgsmål 2 (7%) — Equivalent to $p \Rightarrow \neg q$

1. **Problem.** Which of 2.a–2.l are logically equivalent to $p\Rightarrow\neg q$?
2. **Type.** Logical equivalence.
3. **Method.** (1) Rewrite the target with $A\Rightarrow B \equiv \neg A\lor B$: here $p\Rightarrow\neg q \equiv \neg p\lor\neg q \equiv \neg(p\land q)$. (2) It is true except when $p$ and $q$ both true. (3) Reduce each candidate the same way (or build a 4-row truth table) and compare. Swap in your own target formula and repeat.
4. **Worked solution.** Target $\neg p\lor\neg q = \neg(p\land q)$, false only at $p=q=$T.
   - **2.a** $p\lor\neg q$ — false at $p$F,$q$T; differs. **No.**
   - **2.b** $\neg p\lor\neg q$ — identical. **YES.**
   - **2.c** $p\land\neg q$ — true only $p$T,$q$F; differs. **No.**
   - **2.d** $\neg(p\land q)$ — identical. **YES.**
   - **2.e** $\neg p\lor(p\land\neg q)$ — distribute: $(\neg p\lor p)\land(\neg p\lor\neg q)=\neg p\lor\neg q$. **YES.**
   - **2.f** $(p\land\neg q)\lor(\neg p\land q)=p\oplus q$ — false at $p=q=$F; differs. **No.**
   - **2.g** $(p\lor q)\land(\neg p\lor q)=q$; differs. **No.**
   - **2.h** $\neg q\Rightarrow p = q\lor p$; differs. **No.**
   - **2.i** $q\lor(p\Rightarrow q)$ — at $p$T,$q$F: F$\lor$F = F, but target = T; differs. **No.**
   - **2.j** $(p\Rightarrow\neg q)\lor(p\land\neg p) = (p\Rightarrow\neg q)\lor\text{F} = p\Rightarrow\neg q$. **YES.**
   - **2.k** $(p\oplus q)\lor\neg q$ — at $p$T,$q$T: F$\lor$F = F = target; at $p$F,$q$F: F$\lor$T=T=target; full check matches. **YES.**
   - **2.l** $\neg p\land(p\oplus q)$ — true only $p$F,$q$T; differs. **No.**
   - **Equivalent: 2.b, 2.d, 2.e, 2.j, 2.k.** (checked: Python)

## Spørgsmål 3 (7%) — Which quantified statements are true?

1. **Problem.** Which of 3.a–3.j hold? Domains as stated ($\mathbb{N}=\{0,1,\dots\}$, $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}^+$).
2. **Type.** Truth of quantified statements over number sets.
3. **Method.** (1) $\exists$: find one witness $\Rightarrow$ true; (2) $\forall$: find one counterexample $\Rightarrow$ false, else argue it holds for all; (3) watch the edge values $n=0,1$ and $\mathbb{N}$ vs $\mathbb{Z}^+$. Swap in your own statement and apply.
4. **Worked solution.**
   - **3.a** $\exists n\in\mathbb{N}:\sqrt n\notin\mathbb{N}$. Take $n=2$, $\sqrt2\notin\mathbb{N}$. **TRUE.**
   - **3.b** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N}:\sqrt n\in\mathbb{N}$. $n=2$ breaks it. **FALSE.**
   - **3.c** $\forall n:n^2\ge n$. $n=0:0\ge0$; $n=1:1\ge1$; $n\ge2$ clearly. **TRUE.**
   - **3.d** $\forall n:n^2>n$. $n=0:0>0$ false (also $n=1$). **FALSE.**
   - **3.e** $\exists n,k:(n+k)^2=25$. Take $n+k=5$ (e.g. $n=2,k=3$). **TRUE.**
   - **3.f** $\exists n:\forall k: k/n\in\mathbb{N}$. Take $n=1$: $k/1=k\in\mathbb{N}$ for all $k$. **TRUE.**
   - **3.g** $\exists n\in\mathbb{Z}^+:\forall k\in\mathbb{Z}^+: n/k<kn$. At $k=1$: need $n<n$, false for every $n$. **FALSE.**
   - **3.h** $\exists n\in\mathbb{Z}:\forall k\in\mathbb{Z}:4\mid(4n+k)$. $4\mid 4n$ always, so need $4\mid k$ for all $k$ — fails ($k=1$). **FALSE.**
   - **3.i** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N}:\exists k\in\mathbb{N}:k=n/2$. Odd $n$ (e.g. $n=1$) gives $n/2\notin\mathbb{N}$. **FALSE.**
   - **3.j** $\forall n,k\in\mathbb{N}:n+k\in\mathbb{N}$. Sum of naturals is natural. **TRUE.**
   - **True: 3.a, 3.c, 3.e, 3.f, 3.j.** (checked: Python)

## Spørgsmål 4 (2%) — Negation of a quantified statement

1. **Problem.** Which is equivalent to $\neg(\exists x\in\mathbb{Z}:\forall y\in\mathbb{Z}: xy=x+y)$?
2. **Type.** Pushing negation through quantifiers.
3. **Method.** Flip each quantifier and negate the core: $\neg\exists\to\forall$, $\neg\forall\to\exists$, and $\neg(xy=x+y)\to xy\ne x+y$. Swap in your own statement and flip top-down.
4. **Worked solution.** $\neg\exists x\forall y(xy=x+y)\equiv\forall x\,\exists y\,(xy\ne x+y)$. **Answer: 4.f.** (checked: Python — negation rule)

## Spørgsmål 5 (12%) — Valid induction proofs of $2^n+3^n<4^n$ for $n\ge2$

1. **Problem.** Claim $2^n+3^n<4^n$ for $n\ge2$. Which of 5.a–5.g are valid induction proofs?
2. **Type.** Judging correctness of induction arguments (base case + inductive step).
3. **Method.** A proof is valid only if: (1) base case matches the claim's start ($n=2$ here), and (2) the inductive step assumes $P(n)$ and *derives* $P(n+1)$ forward, using only correct inequalities — never starting from the goal and working back to the hypothesis, and never mis-applying algebra. Check each: right base? forward direction? every inequality justified? Swap in your own claim/steps.
4. **Worked solution.** Verified $2^n+3^n<4^n$ first holds at $n=2$ ($13<16$), and fails at $n=0,1$ ($2<1$ false, $5<4$ false); correct base is $n=2$. (checked: Python)
   - **5.a** Base $n=2$ OK. Step multiplies IH by 4: $4(2^n+3^n)<4\cdot4^n$, then jumps to $2^{n+1}+3^{n+1}<4^{n+1}$. But $4(2^n+3^n)\ne 2^{n+1}+3^{n+1}$ (it equals $2^{n+2}+\dots$); the final implication is unjustified. **Invalid.**
   - **5.b** Base $n=3$, but claim starts at $n=2$ — wrong base case. **Invalid.**
   - **5.c** Base $n=2$, step $n-1\to n$. Same flawed algebra as 5.a: $4(2^{n-1}+3^{n-1})\ne 2^n+3^n$, last line not justified. **Invalid.**
   - **5.d** Step starts from goal $2^n+3^n<4^n$ and derives the hypothesis $2^{n-1}+3^{n-1}<4^{n-1}$ — backwards, proves nothing. **Invalid.**
   - **5.e** Step starts from $2^{n+1}+3^{n+1}<4^{n+1}$ (the goal) and derives $2^n+3^n<4^n$ — assumes the conclusion. **Invalid.**
   - **5.f** Base $n=2$ OK. Step forward: $2^{n+1}+3^{n+1}=2\cdot2^n+3\cdot3^n<3(2^n+3^n)$ (since $2\cdot2^n<3\cdot2^n$) $<3\cdot4^n$ (IH) $<4\cdot4^n=4^{n+1}$. Every inequality correct, direction forward. **Valid.**
   - **5.g** Writes $2^n+3^n=\tfrac12 2^{n+1}+\tfrac13 3^{n+1}$, claims $<\tfrac13(2^{n+1}+3^{n+1})$ — but $\tfrac12>\tfrac13$, inequality goes the wrong way, and it applies IH at $n+1$ which isn't assumed. **Invalid.**
   - **Valid: 5.f only.** (checked: Python — base case and inequality directions)

## Spørgsmål 6 (8%) — Properties of relation $S$ from its graph

1. **Problem.** Graph on $\{a,b,c,d,e,f\}$ representing $S$. Which of reflexive / symmetric / antisymmetric / transitive / equivalence / partial order / total order hold? Edges read from the diagram: self-loops on $a,b,e,f$; directed $a\to b, a\to c, a\to e, a\to d$; $b\to e$; $c\to d$; $e\leftrightarrow f$ (both directions); $f\to d$.
2. **Type.** Relation-property check from a directed graph.
3. **Method.** (1) Reflexive: every vertex has a self-loop. (2) Symmetric: every arrow has its reverse. (3) Antisymmetric: no pair of distinct vertices with arrows both ways. (4) Transitive: $x\to y, y\to z \Rightarrow x\to z$. (5) Equivalence = reflexive + symmetric + transitive; partial order = reflexive + antisymmetric + transitive; total order = partial order where every two elements are comparable. Swap in your own graph's edges.
4. **Worked solution.**
   - **6.a Reflexive?** $c$ and $d$ have no self-loop. **FALSE.**
   - **6.b Symmetric?** $a\to b$ but no $b\to a$. **FALSE.**
   - **6.c Antisymmetric?** $e\to f$ and $f\to e$ with $e\ne f$. **FALSE.**
   - **6.d Transitive?** $e\to f$ and $f\to d$ but no $e\to d$ (also $a\to e,e\to f$ no $a\to f$). **FALSE.**
   - **6.e Equivalence?** Needs reflexive+symmetric+transitive — all fail. **FALSE.**
   - **6.f Partial order?** Needs reflexive+antisymmetric+transitive — all fail. **FALSE.**
   - **6.g Total order?** Not even a partial order. **FALSE.**
   - **All false (6.a–6.g).** (checked: Python — relation-property checker on the diagram edges.) Disqualifiers: missing self-loops on $c,d$ (kills reflexive), $a\to b$ without $b\to a$ (kills symmetric), $e\leftrightarrow f$ both ways (kills antisymmetric), $e\to f,f\to d$ but no $e\to d$ (kills transitive).

## Spørgsmål 7 (3%) — Symmetric closure

1. **Problem.** Give the symmetric closure (symmetriske lukning) of $T=\{(a,b),(b,b),(c,d),(d,e)\}$.
2. **Type.** Closure of a relation.
3. **Method.** Symmetric closure $= T\cup T^{-1}$: keep all original pairs and add the reverse $(y,x)$ of every $(x,y)$. Do NOT add self-loops (that's reflexive closure) or chains (that's transitive closure). Swap in your own $T$.
4. **Worked solution.** Reverses: $(b,a),(b,b),(d,c),(e,d)$. Union: $\{(a,b),(b,a),(b,b),(c,d),(d,c),(d,e),(e,d)\}$. **Answer: 7.c.** (checked: Python)
